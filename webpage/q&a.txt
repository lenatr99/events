How are German weddings different to American or British ones?
German weddings often feel a bit like a relaxed family festival: lots of food, cake, and time to talk, with fewer big “set pieces” like long speeches or bouquet tosses. The focus is on spending time together rather than a tight timeline of formalities.
Are you having a civil ceremony (Standesamt)?
No. In Germany, couples usually have a separate civil ceremony at the registry office (Standesamt), which is the legal marriage. We are already legally married, so our celebration will be a symbolic ceremony and party rather than a formal civil wedding.
Will there be a church ceremony?
We’re having a Catholic-style wedding ceremony, but in a symbolic, very personal celebration rather than a traditional full Catholic nuptial Mass. The priest (Pfarrer) will speak primarily in German so that the ceremony doesn’t take twice as long by repeating everything in two languages. Key parts like the welcome, readings, short homily, and blessings will be in German, but the rhythm will feel very familiar to anyone who has been to a church wedding before. To make it easy to follow, there will be a service booklet (Kirchenblatt) with English explanations and translations of the main moments, so you can see where we are in the ceremony and what is being said. You are warmly invited to sit, stand, and join in responses or songs as you feel comfortable, or simply follow along quietly and enjoy the atmosphere.
What about speeches?
German weddings usually have fewer formal speeches, and they tend to be shorter and more informal than in the UK or US. We may have a few short toasts, but you can expect more time for chatting, eating, and drinking than sitting through long speeches.
Will there be assigned seating?
Yes, there will be a seating plan for the meal, as is common at German weddings. After dinner, guests usually move around more freely between tables, bar, and dance floor.

What about the schedule – when does it end?
German wedding parties often go late into the night, and it’s normal for guests to stay well past midnight. You’re free to head to bed whenever you like, but if you enjoy late-night dancing and dessert, you’ll fit right in. There are a limited number of guest rooms in the castle that can be booked for 75€. While you can stay there over night, please keep in mind that there are only shared bathrooms on the hall. According to the Duke and Duchess who are hosting us, guests typically book these rooms to have a place to lay down for a moment, for kids to go to bed early before parents are ready to leave, or somewhere to change. Please let us know if you're interested in one of these.
What is “Kaffee und Kuchen”?
In Germany, “Kaffee und Kuchen” is a beloved afternoon tradition that’s a bit like a relaxed tea time with coffee and cake. Instead of one big wedding cake moment, guests enjoy a buffet of homemade cakes, coffee, and conversation, often over a couple of unhurried hours.
What does the wedding day schedule look like?
Our ceremony will be at 2 pm, followed by a short drinks reception right outside. After that, there will be some downtime while we take photos, and guests are welcome to explore the castle, join a tour, or wander the grounds at their own pace. Later in the afternoon, we’ll gather again for Kaffee und Kuchen with a buffet of homemade cakes, followed by dinner, more drinks, and dancing into the night.
Is there a fixed timeline for everything?
Not really. German weddings tend to be more relaxed and less strictly timed than many American or British weddings. Things will happen in a natural flow rather than to-the-minute scheduling, so feel free to settle in, get a coffee, and enjoy the day as it unfolds.
What kind of music will the DJ play?
The DJ will play a mix of German party music and international songs, including plenty of familiar English-language tracks. The overall vibe will be more like a German party playlist than a typical American wedding set list, so you might hear some new favorites alongside classics you already know.
Will there be ballroom dancing?
Yes! There will be time and space for ballroom-style dancing as well as more casual party dancing. If you know a waltz or foxtrot, you’ll definitely have a chance to use it, but you’ll also be totally fine just dancing however you like.
Do people in Germany speak English?
Yes, many people in Germany speak English, especially younger people and those working in hotels, restaurants, and tourist areas. In cities and tourist spots you can usually get by in English, though learning a few basic German phrases (hello, please, thank you) is very much appreciated.
Do we need to rent a car?
You don’t have to rent a car, but it can make things easier. Trains and buses in Germany are generally reliable and comfortable, especially between larger towns and cities, but a car gives you more flexibility for small villages, castles, and countryside outings at your own pace.
Are there Ubers in Germany?
The Uber app works but connects you with license taxis rather than private cars. Generally speaking, you can use apps such as Uber and Bolt to request rides, and there are also Germany‑wide taxi apps (like Taxi Deutschland, taxi.eu or FREE NOW) that connect you directly with local cabs. We recommend downloading at least one ride‑hailing app and one traditional taxi app before you travel, so you have options if coverage is limited in smaller towns.
